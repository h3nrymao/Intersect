Dutch Disease and Taiwan’s Chip Dependency

Henry Mao|1 June 2026|5 min read

My Time in Groningen

Walking through the streets of Groningen earlier this year, it is hard to imagine that this quiet university city in the north of the Netherlands was once the epicentre of an economic phenomenon so significant that it gave rise to its own term in economics, coined the “Dutch Disease”.

Beneath the cobblestones and canals lies what was once the largest natural gas field in western Europe, estimated at 2,800 billion cubic metres. The Groningen gas field, discovered in 1959, generated over 363 billion euros in revenue for the Dutch state over six decades. It transformed the Netherlands from a modest post-war economy into one of Europe’s wealthiest nations. But it also hollowed out Dutch manufacturing, inflated the currency, and created a dependency that took decades to unwind.

Now, with the field officially shut down and decommissioning underway at an estimated cost of 2 billion euros, the question is no longer about Groningen (NL Times, 2026). It is about who might be next.

Dutch Disease Explained

The term “Dutch Disease” was coined by The Economist in 1977, but formalised by Corden and Neary (1982). A boom in one export sector draws in foreign currency, lifts the real exchange rate, and pulls labour and capital toward the booming sector and services, leaving other tradables, manufacturing above all, to lose competitiveness and decline.

The Netherlands is the main example, with gas revenues causing increased public spending, strengthening the currency, manufacturing lost competitiveness, and by the early 1980s the country was in crisis. The wider pattern is what Sachs and Warner (2001) called the resource curse, where economies richest in a single resource tend to grow more slowly than resource-poor ones.

Taiwan’s Silicon Shield

Six thousand kilometres east of Groningen, Taiwan presents a strikingly parallel situation, though with microchips rather than natural gas.

Taiwan Semiconductor Manufacturing Company (TSMC) accounts for roughly 8% of the island’s economic output, 12% of its exports, over 40% of the Taiex benchmark and produces over 90% of the world’s most advanced semiconductors, the chips behind everything from smartphones to artificial intelligence.

This dominance underpins what the journalist Craig Addison (2001) called Taiwan’s “Silicon Shield”, where the global reliance on Taiwanese chips deters military aggression, since any disruption to TSMC would send shockwaves through the world economy.

Two-Dimensional Problem

Taiwan’s economy is increasingly two-dimensional: a globally dominant semiconductor sector on one side and on the other everything else, from traditional manufacturing to services and agriculture, struggling for talent and capital in an economy oriented around one industry.

The parallel is not exact, since Taiwan’s resource is made rather than extracted. Yet Corden and Neary (1982) anticipated this, noting that their model applies equally where the booming sector is not extractive but simply more advanced.

Corden and Neary (1982)’s two mechanisms map onto Taiwan. The resource-movement effect draws the best engineers toward chips, starving other industries of talent. The spending effect should run through the currency, but this part of the analogy frays. The model predicts a rising exchange rate, yet the New Taiwan Dollar weakened through 2024 and, even after a sharp 2025 rally, stays structurally undervalued against a vast trade surplus held down by central-bank intervention and insurers’ unhedged dollar positions (Setser, 2026). The appreciation that hollowed out Dutch manufacturing has so far been resisted in Taipei. However, strategic dependency is undiluted; any major disruption would convulse the whole economy.

The Geopolitical Dimension

What makes Taiwan’s case more acute than the Netherlands’ is the geopolitical overlay. Its semiconductor concentration is both an economic vulnerability and a geopolitical flashpoint.

China’s claims are not merely territorial but economical. Control of TSMC’s fabrication plants would hand any country extraordinary leverage over global technology. The United States, meanwhile, is reshoring production through the CHIPS and Science Act. Both pressures erode Taiwan’s position.

Successful diversification weakens the Silicon Shield, while any disruption to TSMC, even short of invasion such as a naval quarantine, would cascade through every economy that depends on advanced computing.

Lessons from Groningen

Standing in Groningen, you see what happens when a one-resource economy reaches the end of its cycle. The gas is gone, extraction earthquakes have damaged thousands of homes and the region that produced the wealth is now among the country’s poorest.

But reinvention is possible too. In my site visit to BioBTX in Groningen, we saw an attempt to build a new industry on the bones of the old. We spoke to Mart Hendriksen, a process technologist at BioBTX who spoke to the importance of “greening the chemical industry with renewable building blocks.” Using catalytic pyrolysis, BioBTX turns waste plastic and biomass into benzene, toluene and xylene, the aromatic building blocks still drawn mostly from crude oil, and has raised over 80 million euros toward a first commercial plant at Delfzijl (BioBTX, 2026). It is hopeful but small and late, arriving decades after the wealth that might have funded it was already spent.

Taiwan is not there yet; its industry is thriving and TSMC’s lead remains formidable. But the lesson from Groningen is that the time to diversify is while the resource still flows, not after it stops. However, the analogy is not airtight, because chips are made, not mined, and the currency has so far moved against the Dutch pattern. Yet the warning survives in spirit. Economic strength built on a single pillar is fragility dressed up as prosperity, and whether that lesson applies to silicon as it did to gas is a question Taiwan cannot afford to answer too late.
