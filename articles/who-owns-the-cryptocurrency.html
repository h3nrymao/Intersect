Who Owns the Cryptocurrency? Recalibrating Custody Regulation for Insolvency Risk and Proprietary Protection

Henry Mao|1 July 2026|13 min read

I. Introduction

The global cryptocurrency market capitalisation sits at US$2.3 trillion.1 But cryptocurrencies like Bitcoin only have 12% held in exchanges and have been in gradual decline since 2019.2 This is likely attributed to the collapse of Celsius, Voyager, FTX, BlockFi and Genesis in 2022, which left customers unable to access their funds, leading to product distrust.3 The exchange FTX alone owed more than US$3 billion to unsecured creditors.4 The critical question in these scenarios is: when the platform has collapsed, who owns the cryptocurrency?

In the Corporations Amendment (Digital Assets Framework) Act 2026 (Cth) (CADAF), Australia is modernising its digital asset regulatory framework by including digital asset and tokenised custody platforms as financial products,5 bringing operators within the Australian Financial Services (AFS) regime,6 and allowing ASIC to make standards on the activities and conduct of these platforms.7 However, the proprietary question of whether a customer has a personal claim or retains ownership in cryptocurrency held on an exchange remains comparatively unexplored.

Australian reforms are miscalibrated on consumer protection. By regulating platform conduct while leaving the property question uncharacterised, CADAF and ASIC set the protective standard too low. Cryptocurrency held on an exchange should warrant a higher bar than a fiat currency in a bank; it is more volatile, exchanges are more prone to insolvency, and customers enjoy no equivalent safety net. Therefore, it is proposed that cryptocurrency held in exchanges should be held on trust by statutory default.

This essay proceeds as follows. Section II sets out the framework and its silence on ownership. Section III argues this silence matters most in insolvency, where proprietary rights better protect customers. Section IV contends a trust is doctrinally available, drawing on Re Blockchain Tech and China Life, while confronting Re Taylor. Section V rebuts the banking analogy. Section VI proposes the default trust model.

II. Australia's Digital Asset Framework

A. CADAF: Regulating Conduct but Not Ownership

The CADAF brings digital asset platforms (DAP) and tokenised custody platforms (TCP) within the definition of a financial product through introducing s764A(1)(la)-(lb) of the Corporations Act 2001 (Cth).8 A DAP is a facility where an operator holds digital tokens for others, like a centralised exchange or a brokerage platform.9 A TCP is a facility in which an operator holds a tokenised non-monetary asset that grants the right to redeem or deliver, like tokenised securities.10 Operators of either must hold an AFS license and comply with the general obligations unless they are for non-financial purposes.11 This includes allowing ASIC to set standards for the activity and conduct of these platforms under s912BE.12

These are conduct obligations. They manage what platforms must comply with while they operate, which includes general obligations under s912A,13 platform rules,14 asset-holding standards,15 transactional and settlement standards,16 etc. Although CADAF states that any money held under a DAP or TCP is to be held on trust on behalf of the client,17 this does not extend to cryptocurrencies. Section 761GC and 761GD explain that DAP and TCP include an underlying asset where an operator acts as a trustee, bailee or dealt with by instruction.18 Effectively, CADAF contemplates trust, bailment or mere instruction, but makes no distinction, leaving the proprietary relationship uncharacterised.19

B. ASIC Regulation and Trust Within Limits

ASIC’s current custody regime already require their licensed asset holders to hold client assets on trust, separated from the holder’s own property.20 However, when cryptocurrencies are held under a platform, that trust obligation only extends to where the cryptocurrency is a financial product.21 ASIC treats the custody of digital asset to be a financial service when ‘some or all of them are financial products’;22 therefore, any cryptocurrency that is a non-financial product falls outside this trust regime. For example, Bitcoin is held on exchanges, but it is not considered a financial product and sits outside the trust scheme.23

For these digital assets, ASIC has merely suggested that AFS licensees may consider the operational requirements in RG 133.24 This includes measures for ‘good practice’ under RG 133.141, like segregation on the blockchain.25 Even where there is a trust obligation, RG 133 exempts omnibus accounts from the duty to hold client assets separately, permitting the client’s assets to commingle.26 The trust survives, but the clients each hold beneficial interests in a commingled pool, making portions harder to identify. Australia’s digital asset framework is therefore binding on conduct, but merely suggestive on ownership.

ASIC’s trust obligations only extend to money and financial product crypto,27 while CADAF leaves ownership up to general law,28 which decides proprietary status case-by-case with reference to each platform’s conduct, causing identically regulated platforms to fail their customers in entirely different ways, as Section III explains.

III. The Insolvency Test

A. Why We Need Proprietary Rights Not Conduct Regulation

Conduct regulation protects customers only when a platform is solvent. It regulates how digital assets are stored, disclosed and transacted, but it has no use when the platform fails and goes into insolvency. When a platform goes insolvent, and there is a need for liquidation, remaining assets get paid out in the order established under s556 of the Corporations Act 2001 (Cth), where it lists out debts and claims that must be paid out before all other unsecured debts.29 However, if the cryptocurrency on the platform is held on trust, it does not form part of the pool distributed to creditors, and gets returned to the customer before the winding-up procedures listed above.30 If it is not, the customer is merely an unsecured creditor, and they will only get compensated after what survives the secured and priority claims, which in most cases is minimal.31 In an unpredictable market, the distinction between a proprietary right or merely a personal right decides whether the customer gets compensated or not.

B. Lessons from the Collapses

Although these examples are in different jurisdictions, the concept remains unchanged; recent collapses confirm this proposition. When FTX collapsed, there was a shortfall or more than US$3 Billion to unsecured creditors, where customers were left uncompensated.32 In Celsius, the US Bankruptcy Court held the platform’s terms of use had passed title in ‘Earn’ account deposits to Celsius itself, which meant the assets fell into the estate and customers were unsecured creditors.33 In Re Gatecoin,34 the court in Hong Kong accepted that cryptocurrency is capable of being held on trust,35 and the exchange’s earlier terms had created one,36 but found the updated terms in 2018 created none,37 taking out the previous trust language and disclaiming fiduciary obligations,38 which meant that the customer’s cryptocurrencies fell into the pool of general creditors.39

Each platform was differently operated and structured, but the outcome was decided on the same principle, whether the property is owned by the platform or by the customer. If a court deems there to not be a trust relationship, the ownership stays with the platform, and the customer is an unsecured creditor.40 This is the risk conduct regulation leaves up to interpretation, which is what a statutory trust aims to remove.

IV. Trust Over Customer Crypto: Doctrine and Its Limits

A. The Three Certainties

A trust requires three certainties: intention, subject matter and objects.41 For cryptocurrencies held on an exchange, subject matter and objects are likely non-contentious. Cryptocurrencies are considered ‘property’, whether it is recorded on a public ledger like the blockchain or, as in Ruscoe, on a platform’s internal SQL database,42 and the beneficiaries are the account holders with their respective positive balances.43 The main contention is certainty of intention, which asks the question of whether there could be a manifestation of intent to hold the cryptocurrencies for the customer or merely to owe it back.44

B. Cryptocurrency as Trust Property

Certainty of intention only matters if cryptocurrency can be considered trust property. Re Blockchain Tech held that an interest in Bitcoin is property, classifying it as a chose in action capable of being held on trust.45 This conclusion rests on settled High Court authority, as Justice Jackman states,46 Cain’s Case established that a chose in action need not be enforceable by court action;47 therefore, cryptocurrency could be recognised as property without reference to the UK ‘third category’ of personal property.48

C. Certainty of Intention and the Objective Test

Intention to create a trust is objectively assessed, from ‘language or conduct showing a sufficiently clear intention’ drawing from the whole circumstance.49 The foundation of which comes from Lord Millet in Twinsectra, where a trust was found from the purpose for which money was transferred rather than an expressed declaration.50 The Hong Kong Court of Final Appeal case China Life explained this further, holding that a Quistclose trust arises where the parties objectively intended that the property is not to be at the recipient’s free disposal,51 and that even when the parties did not intend the transferor to retain a beneficial interest,52 the trust is still in effect. This matters for digital assets because a customer who deposits cryptocurrency on the objective understanding it is held for their account may still invite a resulting trust even in the absence of express trust language.53

D. The Danger: Re Taylor

The difficulty is that courts generally have refused to infer such intention where documents point in the other direction. In Re Taylor, the Singapore High Court declined to find any trust, express, resulting or Quistclose, over unclaimed customer cryptocurrency held by an insolvent exchange.54 The court decided that certainty of intention failed for three reasons.55 Firstly, ‘custodial assets’ is undefined in Singapore law and assets may be held on a pledge, not necessarily proprietary.56 Secondly, clauses in the terms and conditions suggested legal title rested with the customer.57 Thirdly, segregation of individual wallets may be for security or accounting purposes and does not evince an intention to create a trust.58 Re Taylor believed segregation and custodial language are too ambiguous to evince a trust.59

E. The Escape: Ruscoe and Conduct

Re Taylor is not the only outcome. Intention could be inferred from conduct as well as terms.60 In Ruscoe, the New Zealand High Court found a trust precisely from the platform’s conduct.61 Cryptopia (the platform) created the exchange, held private keys and maintained a database recording each account holder’s entitlement, manifesting intention through their conduct.62 Re Gatecoin accepted the proprietary nature of cryptocurrency and ability to be held on trust,63 and agreed the previous terms and conditions would have created one,64 only to be superseded by later terms disclaiming fiduciary obligation,65 defeating the trust.

F. Consequence for Regulation

This shows a trust over customer cryptocurrencies is available doctrinally, the asset qualifies, and the objective-intention test in China Life may capture the ordinary depositing customer.66 However, currently, whether a trust arises largely depends on drafting, where the difference between Ruscoe and Re Taylor was wording in a user agreement. Leaving this consequential distinction up to a contractual agreement that most users do not even read is precisely what consumer-protection regulation should not do. By establishing a statutory default trust, it converts an uncertain litigated outcome into a predictable one, which is the policy calibration CADAF and ASIC lack.

V. The Banking Analogy and Its Limits

A. The Banking Creditor Model

The strongest objection is that exchanges should be treated like banks. When a customer deposits money with a bank, they take title and owe only a personal debt; as Lord Cottenham held, it ‘ceases altogether to be the money of the customer’ and becomes the banker’s, leaving the customer a creditor, not a beneficiary.67 Banking has operated on this basis since the 1800s without proprietary protection for depositors. A cryptocurrency depositor of an exchange, on this view, should be treated the same, and should bear the risk of its insolvency and inability to pay all creditors back. Further, Levitin argues that because commingled coins are fungible and not serialised,68 a court likely analogises the relationship to a general bank deposit, a loan, leaving customers as unsecured creditors.69 The argument would be that a trust singles out cryptocurrency for protection bank depositors do not enjoy, which may unsettle exchanges or bank customers.

B. Why the Banking Analogy Fails

The objection’s loan framing is not fatal. A Quistclose trust could arise out of a loan;70 characterisation of the deposit as a loan does not make the customer a mere creditor. However, this analogy fails for three further reasons.

Firstly, the creditor model works well for banks because it sits inside a prudential regulation that crypto platforms lack. Authorised deposit-taking institutions (ADIs) are supervised by APRA under the Banking Act 1959 (Cth),71 and their depositors are protected by the Financial Claims Scheme,72 which guarantees deposits for up to AU$250,000 per account holder per ADI.73 The depositor may be an unsecured creditor at law, but a protected one in practice. Cryptocurrency customers do not enjoy this benefit; there are no regulated structures, protective schemes, nor customer guarantees. To import the bank’s creditor model without its protection leaves customers bearing the risk while stripped of the remedy.

Secondly, the analogy does not fit when looking at customer expectation. Foley relied on the depositor knowingly allowing the bank to use the deposited money as its own, which a trustee never could.74 The cryptocurrency exchange customer expect the opposite, that the coins are held for them, not given for the platform to, in Lord Cottenham’s words, ‘do with it as he pleases’.75

Thirdly, a default trust does not abolish commercial flexibility. The main function of a cryptocurrency exchange is acting as an intermediary, allowing users to trade and convert digital assets, remains untouched.76 The trade-off is minimal: customers will still be able to execute trades as usual, but platforms that lend cannot do so unless customers express their consent. In return, customers gain proprietary protection,77 and in the event of insolvency, customers recover their assets more readily.78

VI. Concluding Remarks: A Proposal for a Trust-by-Default Model

This essay has argued that Australia’s digital asset reforms are miscalibrated on consumer protection. By regulating conduct while leaving ownership uncharacterised, they fail to address the insolvency question adequately. The remedy is to legislate the solution. Regulators have three options. The first, the current model, is sticking to the status quo and leaving the proprietary status to the platform’s terms and courts. The second, the disclosure model, is to require platforms to state explicitly whether a customer’s given asset is held on trust. The third, the trust by default model, holds the customer’s cryptocurrency on trust unless the customer expressly and separately agrees otherwise.

The analysis does not explicitly suggest that Parliament must mandate the third model but aims to consider all options and their consequences, arguing the trust by default model best answers policy calibration. The current model has already failed, as the collapses show. The disclosure model improves transparency between consumers and exchanges but leaves the proprietary categorisation untouched; a passive customer remains exposed to unsuccessful outcomes in Re Taylor and Re Gatecoin.79 Only a default trust model protects idle customers, while preserving flexibility for platforms, from custodial TCPs, where trust is natural, 80 to pooled DAPs, where the risk is most prominent81.

The practical implications would be immediate: platforms would review terms against China Life’s objectively inferred trust,82 regulators would address the proprietary characterisation, not just conduct, and customers would be protected. ASIC’s standard-making power under s912BE provides a starting point for regulators,83 and China Life‘s persuasive framing will likely be accepted into Australia’s court,84 because former High Court of Australia justice Gummow J contributed to the decision. Still strong persuasive authority must await the right case before the right court before it becomes binding precedent. That uncertainty is precisely why regulators should legislate now rather than wait. Australia already accepts cryptocurrency as property; it should now decide whose.
