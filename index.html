From Groningen to Taipei: What the Dutch Disease Tells Us About Taiwan's Semiconductor Dependency

Henry Mao|1 May 2026|9 min read

Standing in Groningen

Walking through the streets of Groningen earlier this year, it is hard to imagine that this quiet university city in the north of the Netherlands was once the epicentre of an economic phenomenon so significant that it gave rise to its own term in economics: the Dutch Disease.

Beneath the cobblestones and canals lies what was once the largest natural gas field in western Europe, estimated at 2,800 billion cubic metres. The Groningen gas field, discovered in 1959, generated over 363 billion euros in revenue for the Dutch state over six decades. It transformed the Netherlands from a modest post-war economy into one of Europe's wealthiest nations. But it also hollowed out Dutch manufacturing, inflated the currency, and created a dependency that took decades to unwind.

Now, with the field officially shut down and decommissioning underway at an estimated cost of 2 billion euros, the question is no longer about Groningen. It is about who might be next.

The Dutch Disease, Explained

The term "Dutch Disease" was coined by The Economist in 1977 to describe a counterintuitive problem: how the discovery of a valuable natural resource can actually harm an economy. The mechanism is straightforward. When a country begins exporting a high-value resource, foreign currency flows in. This strengthens the domestic currency, making the country's other exports, such as manufactured goods, more expensive on the global market. Those industries lose competitiveness, shrink, and eventually decline.

In the Netherlands, gas revenues funded an expansion of the welfare state and public spending through the 1970s. Dutch labour costs rose, the guilder strengthened, and manufacturing firms found themselves unable to compete internationally. By the early 1980s, the country was in a deep financial crisis. The disease was named retrospectively, but the pattern was unmistakable: a one-dimensional economy built on a single dominant export sector, vulnerable to the moment that sector falters.

Taiwan's Silicon Shield

Six thousand kilometres east of Groningen, Taiwan presents a strikingly parallel situation, though with silicon wafers rather than natural gas.

Taiwan Semiconductor Manufacturing Company (TSMC) is responsible for approximately 8% of Taiwan's total economic output and 12% of its exports. The company produces over 90% of the world's most advanced semiconductors, the chips that power everything from smartphones to artificial intelligence systems. Taiwan's stock market has surged to repeated record highs on the back of the AI boom, with TSMC at the centre of the rally.

This dominance has been described as Taiwan's "Silicon Shield," the theory that the world's dependence on Taiwanese chips provides a form of strategic protection against Chinese aggression, since any disruption to TSMC's operations would send shockwaves through the entire global economy.

But there is another way to read Taiwan's situation: as a textbook case of resource concentration risk, not unlike the Netherlands in the 1970s.

The Two-Dimensional Problem

Taiwan's economy is increasingly two-dimensional. On one side sits a globally dominant semiconductor sector, attracting investment, talent, and government attention. On the other sits everything else: traditional manufacturing, services, agriculture, and small businesses that struggle to compete for resources and skilled workers in an economy oriented around one industry.

The parallels to the Dutch Disease are not exact. Taiwan's "resource" is not extracted from the ground but manufactured through extraordinary technological capability. TSMC's dominance is the product of decades of strategic investment, engineering excellence, and government policy, not geological luck. But the structural vulnerability is similar.

When a single sector dominates an economy to this degree, several risks emerge. Talent concentration means the best engineers and graduates are drawn to semiconductors, starving other industries of human capital. Currency effects mirror the classic Dutch Disease: strong demand for Taiwanese exports in semiconductors strengthens the New Taiwan Dollar, making other exports less competitive. And strategic dependency means that any disruption, whether from geopolitical conflict, technological disruption, or a shift in global supply chains, would have outsized consequences for the entire economy.

The Geopolitical Dimension

What makes Taiwan's situation more acute than the Netherlands' is the geopolitical overlay. The Dutch Disease was an economic problem with economic consequences. Taiwan's semiconductor concentration is both an economic vulnerability and a geopolitical flashpoint.

China's claims over Taiwan are not merely territorial; they are geoeconomic. Control of TSMC's fabrication facilities would give Beijing extraordinary leverage over the global technology supply chain. Meanwhile, the United States has responded by investing heavily in domestic chip manufacturing through the CHIPS Act, seeking to reduce dependence on Taiwanese production. Both moves, in different ways, threaten to erode Taiwan's position.

If the world successfully diversifies semiconductor manufacturing away from Taiwan, the Silicon Shield weakens. And if China were ever to disrupt TSMC's operations, even through measures short of invasion such as a naval quarantine, the economic shock would cascade through every sector of the global economy that depends on advanced computing.

Lessons from Groningen

Standing in Groningen, you can see what happens when a one-resource economy reaches the end of its cycle. The gas is gone. The earthquakes caused by extraction have damaged thousands of homes. The region that produced the wealth is now one of the poorest in the Netherlands, awaiting compensation and reinvention.

Taiwan is not there yet. Its semiconductor industry is thriving, and TSMC's technological lead remains formidable. But the lesson from Groningen is that the time to diversify is when the resource is still flowing, not after it stops. The Netherlands spent its gas revenues on welfare rather than building alternative industries. The question for Taiwan is whether it will use its semiconductor boom to build economic resilience or allow the same structural dependency to calcify.

The Dutch Disease teaches us that economic strength built on a single pillar is not strength at all. It is fragility dressed up as prosperity. Whether that lesson applies to silicon as much as it did to natural gas is a question Taiwan cannot afford to answer too late.

The AI Arms Race: How Artificial Intelligence Is Redrawing the Geopolitical Map

Henry Mao|1 April 2026|8 min read

A New Kind of Strategic Competition

For most of the twentieth century, geopolitical power was measured in warheads, aircraft carriers, and barrels of oil. In 2026, a new metric has emerged: compute. The nations that control the development, deployment, and governance of artificial intelligence are positioning themselves to shape the global order for decades to come.

The AI race is no longer a technology story. It is a geopolitical one. And the implications extend far beyond Silicon Valley and Shenzhen.

The US-China AI Rivalry

At the centre of this competition sits the US-China rivalry. The United States maintains a significant lead in frontier AI research, driven by companies like OpenAI, Anthropic, Google DeepMind, and Meta. American firms benefit from access to the world's most advanced semiconductor manufacturing (primarily through Taiwan's TSMC), deep capital markets, and a research ecosystem that attracts global talent.

China, meanwhile, has pursued a different strategy. Beijing's "New Generation Artificial Intelligence Development Plan," first published in 2017, set the explicit goal of becoming the world leader in AI by 2030. The Chinese government has backed this ambition with massive state investment, access to vast domestic datasets, and integration of AI into military and surveillance applications at a scale unmatched elsewhere.

The result is a bifurcating AI ecosystem. American export controls on advanced chips, particularly the restrictions on NVIDIA's high-end GPUs and ASML's extreme ultraviolet lithography machines, have sought to limit China's access to the computational infrastructure required for training frontier models. China has responded by accelerating domestic chip development and finding creative workarounds, including stockpiling chips before restrictions took effect and developing architectures optimised for less advanced hardware.

AI as a Tool of Statecraft

Beyond the bilateral rivalry, AI is reshaping how states project power. Three areas stand out.

First, intelligence and surveillance. AI-powered facial recognition, natural language processing, and satellite imagery analysis have transformed the capabilities of intelligence agencies worldwide. Nations with advanced AI can monitor adversaries, predict political instability, and detect military movements with unprecedented speed and precision.

Second, information warfare. Generative AI has dramatically lowered the cost of producing convincing disinformation. Deepfakes, synthetic news articles, and AI-generated social media personas can be deployed at scale to influence elections, undermine trust in institutions, and destabilise adversaries.

Third, autonomous military systems. The development of AI-powered drones, autonomous weapons, and AI-assisted command systems is changing the calculus of military conflict. The use of AI-guided drones in recent conflicts has provided a glimpse of how future warfare may be fought, raising profound legal and ethical questions about accountability and the laws of armed conflict.

The Governance Vacuum

Perhaps the most consequential dimension of AI geopolitics is the absence of a coherent global governance framework. Unlike nuclear weapons, which are governed by a web of treaties and norms, AI development operates in a regulatory vacuum at the international level.

The European Union has moved furthest with the EU AI Act, establishing a risk-based regulatory framework that categorises AI applications by their potential for harm. The United States has taken a more industry-led approach, relying on executive orders and voluntary commitments from major AI companies. China has implemented its own regulations, including rules on deepfakes and algorithmic recommendation systems, though these serve both consumer protection and state control objectives.

The fundamental challenge is that AI governance requires international cooperation between nations that view AI as a strategic competition. The tension between collaboration (needed to manage shared risks like autonomous weapons and AI-driven misinformation) and competition (driven by the economic and military advantages AI confers) remains unresolved.

What This Means for the Rest of the World

For nations outside the US-China duopoly, the AI revolution presents a difficult strategic question. Middle powers like Australia, the United Kingdom, Japan, and the EU member states must decide how to position themselves: whether to align closely with the American AI ecosystem, pursue strategic autonomy, or attempt to play a bridging role.

For developing economies, the stakes are equally high but the options more constrained. AI threatens to automate precisely the kinds of manufacturing and service-sector jobs that have historically driven economic development. At the same time, AI could leapfrog traditional development pathways in areas like healthcare, agriculture, and education, but only if the infrastructure, skills, and institutional frameworks are in place.

The Road Ahead

The AI arms race is still in its early stages. The models being trained today will look primitive within a decade. But the geopolitical structures being built around AI, the alliances, the export controls, the regulatory frameworks, and the military doctrines, will prove far more durable than any particular technology. The decisions being made now about who controls AI, who governs it, and who benefits from it will shape the balance of power for a generation.

Inside Hong Kong's Notarial System: How Documents Cross Borders

Henry Mao|1 March 2026|5 min read

Why Notarization Matters in a Globalised Economy

In a world where parties transact across borders without ever meeting, how do you trust that a document is genuine? That question sits at the heart of notarial practice, and having worked as a notary clerk at Ravenscroft & Schmierer in Hong Kong, I've seen firsthand how the answer plays out daily.

A notary public is fundamentally an authenticator. They verify that a document was genuinely executed, voluntarily and with full understanding, then certify its authenticity for international use. It sounds straightforward, but in a financial hub like Hong Kong where international business is the lifeblood of the economy, this function is critical infrastructure.

This article is adapted from a longer piece I wrote for Ravenscroft & Schmierer. You can read the full original article here.

The Practical Reality

Consider the most common scenario: a Hong Kong resident needs to sell property overseas. Their foreign lawyer needs proof that the power of attorney is genuine and was executed by the actual property owner. A Hong Kong notary public provides exactly that assurance.

Similarly, individuals opening bank accounts abroad must prove their identity and address. As a clerk, I've helped certify proofs of address through bank statements and utility receipts, and I've witnessed signatures by creating authenticated records that foreign financial institutions recognise and trust. Beyond individual transactions, notaries authenticate business documents, witness agreements, and take statutory declarations for legal proceedings abroad.

What distinguishes notaries from lawyers in the traditional sense is their impartiality. Notaries do not represent clients or appear in court. As neutral third parties, they serve all parties equally, which is foundational to their credibility.

How Documents Go Global: The Apostille Process

After notarization, documents destined for use in another country often require an additional layer of authentication called an apostille. This is a certificate issued by the High Court Registry confirming that the notary is licensed to practice in Hong Kong. It applies to countries that are signatories to the Hague Convention of 1961, covering over 120 jurisdictions worldwide.

The traditional process involves submitting the notarized document to the High Court Registry, paying a fee of HK$125 per document, and allowing two working days for processing. For documents destined for non-Convention countries such as the UAE or Saudi Arabia, a more complex legalisation process through consulates is required.

Hong Kong's Digital Evolution: The E-Apostille

Perhaps the most significant recent development is Hong Kong's adoption of the electronic Apostille Programme. Starting in September 2025, notarized documents can be submitted, processed, and received entirely online. Users can apply for apostille certification through a digital platform, make payments electronically, and receive apostilled documents without visiting the High Court in person.

The e-apostille carries the same legal weight as traditional apostilles across all Hague Convention member countries, ensuring that digital authentication provides complete international recognition. This shift represents a broader trend in legal practice: the digitisation of processes that have remained largely unchanged for decades.

Why This Matters Beyond Hong Kong

The need for notaries reflects a simple reality: distance creates risk in international transactions. Whether you're executing a power of attorney for overseas property, establishing international business agreements, or navigating cross-border personal documents, notarization provides the authentication framework that makes these transactions possible.

In an era of increasing digital fraud and cross-border complexity, the role of the notary, far from becoming obsolete, is arguably more important than ever. The shift toward e-apostilles suggests the profession is adapting rather than declining, modernising its tools while preserving its core function: creating trust between strangers separated by borders.
